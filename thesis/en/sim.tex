\chapter{Partitioning Strategy Benchmark Results}
\label{appx:benchmark}

This appendix presents benchmark results comparing eight partitioning strategies on the \texttt{adr4\_197.qasm} circuit (maximum partition size: 4 qubits).

\section{Benchmark Circuit}
\label{appx:benchmark:circuit}

The \texttt{adr4\_197.qasm} benchmark circuit from RevLib~\cite{revlib2024} represents a reversible arithmetic circuit implementing a 4-bit adder. This circuit provides a realistic test case for partitioning algorithms, containing a mixture of single-qubit and multi-qubit gates with varying connectivity patterns. The circuit exhibits moderate complexity, making it suitable for evaluating both greedy and optimal partitioning approaches within reasonable computation times.

\section{Analysis}
\label{appx:benchmark:observations}

\label{tab:benchmark-results}

The benchmark results reveal clear performance tradeoffs between algorithm categories. Greedy algorithms prioritize execution speed, with Kahn completing in 81 milliseconds to produce 268 partitions and TDAG achieving 211 partitions in 108 milliseconds. These sub-second completion times make greedy approaches suitable for interactive circuit editing workflows where immediate feedback is essential.

Optimal algorithms demonstrate superior partition quality at the cost of longer computation times. ILP achieves the best partition count of 204 in 977 milliseconds through global optimization. GTQCP produces 207 partitions in 105 milliseconds, demonstrating that tree-based optimization can approach optimal quality while maintaining near-greedy speed.

Fusion-aware variants introduce significant complexity variations. ILP-Fusion maintains the same 204 partitions as standard ILP in comparable time at 911 milliseconds, suggesting that fusion cost modeling integrates efficiently into the optimization framework. However, ILP-Fusion-CA drastically increases both partition count to 341 and execution time to 110.3 seconds, illustrating how control-aware cost modeling substantially complicates the optimization problem despite targeting improved simulation efficiency.

Hardware-aware strategies from Qiskit show moderate overhead. Standard Qiskit partitioning produces 207 partitions in 124 milliseconds, performing comparably to GTQCP. Qiskit-Fusion increases both metrics to 239 partitions and 363 milliseconds due to post-processing fusion operations, demonstrating the computational cost of combining hardware constraints with gate fusion optimization.
